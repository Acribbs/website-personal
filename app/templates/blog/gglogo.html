GGSEQLOGO TO PLOT PUBLICATION READY SEQUENCE LOGOS

  • Adam Cribbs
  • Nov 18, 2017

I recently came across the paper "ggseqlogo: a versatile R package for drawing sequence logos" published in Bioinformatics and thought it perfectly explains my reason for moving away from pythons matplotlib in favour of R's ggplot2.

The final and most crucial part of the scientific process is generating figures for publications. When I first began my career as a computational biologist I always struggled with deciding upon the best methods for generating publication ready figures.

I was always torn between generating figures using python's matplotlib and seaborn libraries and R's ggplot2. I began using Matplotlib and seaborn because they offer great flexibility in generating publication quality figures and they integrate very well with pandas data frames, making the whole data analysis more streamlined without having to make intermediate tables and data frames. However, more recently I have chosen to move away from these libraries in favour of R's ggplot2.

My main reason for this is because it can take quite a lot of time think and write code to make a matplotlib figure, this is particularly apparent when you want to make simple less-bespoke plots. I found that using ggplot2 to construct plots was so much more simpler and intuitive and provided a great advantage when creating simple small plots.

Another advantage is that ggplot2 is very versatile and can easily be extended to increase plotting functionality. Hence, my reason for discussing ggplotlogo. ggplotlogo extends the base ggplot package and can create some very high quality plots that can be directly used for publication quality figures. Another nice thing about the package is that it is very nicely explained and documented.

Im using this paper and its associated cpde as a good example of how ggplot2 can be extended and I look forward to making some of my own ggplot2 extensions in the future.
